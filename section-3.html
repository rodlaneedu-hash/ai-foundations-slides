What It's Good At — and Where It Falls Over

Every strength has a flip side. Same mechanics, different outcome depending on how you use it.

It's fast and scalable

It can draft, summarise, translate, or restructure content in seconds. That's genuinely useful.

The flip side

Fast doesn't mean right. Speed comes from predicting the next word — not from checking whether the output is accurate.

It's versatile

It can work across writing, planning, analysis, code, and brainstorming — trained on text spanning many domains.

The flip side

It knows nothing about you — your organisation, your role, your context. Unless you provide that context, every response is generic. This is why the quality of your prompt matters so much.

It's fluent

It produces natural, well-structured language. This is what it's best at — language patterns are exactly what it was trained on.

The flip side

Sounding right and being right are completely unrelated. A confidently written paragraph full of errors looks identical to one that's accurate. You can't tell the difference by reading the tone.

It's always available

It doesn't get tired, it doesn't have a bad day, and there's no wait time.

The flip side

It has no judgment. It can't tell when it's out of its depth. It won't say "I don't know enough" — it'll just produce something plausible and move on.

It recombines ideas in useful ways

Trained on a wide range of text, it can make unexpected connections — suggesting approaches you might not have considered.

The flip side

It's remixing patterns, not inventing. It mixes and matches existing ideas, not comes up with new ones. There's no intent or understanding behind the combinations.

It adapts within a conversation

Give it feedback and it adjusts — change the tone, restructure the format, go deeper on one section.

The flip side

That learning disappears when the conversation ends. Next time you start a chat, it has no memory of what you discussed. It starts fresh every time.

It can work with a lot of information at once

Paste in long documents, data, meeting notes, or policies. Most modern tools handle tens of thousands of words in a single conversation.

The flip side

There's a ceiling. Every tool has a limit. If you have a very long conversation, it quietly forgets the earliest parts — and it won't tell you it's happened.

Baked into the technology's design

The strengths are real — this is a genuinely useful tool.

The limitations aren't bugs that will be fixed in the next update. They're baked into how it works.

Using it well means leaning into what it's good at — while designing around where it falls over.

Human in the loop. Every time.
