Phase 2: Decide

When to Use It
— and When Not To

Turning strengths and limitations
into a practical decision framework.

4
Use it when

First draft. Language. Structure.

  • You need a starting point, not a final product.
    Blank page to working draft in seconds.
  • The task is about language and structure.
    Summarising, reformatting, translating, adjusting tone — these play directly to its strengths.
4
Use it when

You can check the output.

  • You can verify the result.
    You have the expertise to spot what's wrong. AI does the heavy lifting; you do quality control.
  • You want to think through options.
    "Give me five ways I could approach this." Material to react to beats a blank page.
  • The stakes are low to moderate.
    Brainstorming, internal planning, early-stage drafts — mistakes are easy to catch and cheap to fix.
4
Don't use it when

You can't verify the output.

If you don't have the expertise to check the result, you have no way of knowing whether it's useful — or dangerously wrong.

The single biggest risk

It sounds confident whether it's right or wrong. If you can't tell the difference, don't use it.

4
Don't use it when

Personal data. High stakes.

  • The task involves personal or sensitive data.
    What you type may be stored or used for training. Confidential means don't include it.
  • The stakes are high and the output goes straight out.
    Legal documents, medical advice, financial decisions — a plausible-sounding error can cause real harm.
4
Don't use it when

Accuracy required. Judgment about people.

  • You need guaranteed accuracy.
    It doesn't look things up. It doesn't verify. If you need a fact, a citation, or a number — go to the source.
  • The task requires judgment about people.
    Hiring, performance assessments, learning support — these need human understanding of context, relationships, and consequences.
4
Key Landing Point
The decision isn't "should I use AI or not?" — it's "does this task match what AI is good at, and can I manage the limitations?"

If you can verify the output and the stakes allow for imperfection, it's probably a good fit.
If you can't check it or the cost of error is high — keep a human in the driver's seat.
