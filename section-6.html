Phase 2: Protect

Risks and How
to Manage Them

The responsibility layer. Here's what can go wrong
and what to do about it.

1
Risk 1 of 7
Bias

AI learns from human text. That text carries human biases. Western, English-language perspectives dominate. Indigenous knowledge, minority perspectives, and non-English-speaking cultures are underrepresented or missing.

What to do about it

Don't treat AI output as neutral or balanced. Read it with the same critical eye you'd apply to any single source. If the task involves diverse perspectives, bring those perspectives in yourself.

2
Risk 2 of 7
Copyright

Pasting copyrighted material in may breach the Copyright Act 1994. AI can reproduce chunks resembling its training data. Who owns AI-generated content hasn't been tested in NZ courts.

What to do about it

Don't paste in material you don't have the right to share. Treat AI output as a starting point you revise and make your own — not finished work you claim outright. Be upfront about AI's role.

3
Risk 3 of 7
Over-reliance & deskilling

If AI produces every first draft, every summary, every plan — the underlying skills start to fade. Writing, analysis, and critical thinking stay sharp through practice. Stop doing them, and they get rusty.

What to do about it

Be deliberate about when you use AI and when you do the work yourself. Use it to accelerate tasks you already know how to do — not as a substitute for learning. If you can't start without it, that's a signal to step back.

4
Risk 4 of 7
Transparency & trust

When someone reads a document or acts on a recommendation, they assume a human wrote it. If AI shaped the substance and the other person didn't know — that's a trust issue.

What to do about it

Develop a clear standard for when to disclose AI use. Rule of thumb: if the other person's trust depends on believing a human wrote it, you should disclose.

5
Risk 5 of 7
Privacy & data

Anything you type may be stored, logged, or used to train the model. The Privacy Act 2020 applies. The Office of the Privacy Commissioner expects: senior leadership approval, Privacy Impact Assessments, and confirmed data handling before adoption.

What to do about it

Never put personal, confidential, or sensitive information into any AI tool unless you've confirmed the provider doesn't retain it. Conduct a Privacy Impact Assessment before organisational adoption.

6
Risk 6 of 7
Equity & access

The best AI tools sit behind paid subscriptions. Free tools have higher error rates, weaker safety, and fewer features. This creates a two-tier system. If you assume everyone has the same access, you're building in inequality.

What to do about it

Don't assume equal access. If AI is part of a workflow or policy, make sure everyone affected has access to tools that are safe enough. Factor in the cost of equitable access when planning.

7
Risk 7 of 7
Māori data sovereignty

AI models may include mātauranga Māori and te reo Māori scraped without consent. When AI reproduces this material, it does so without understanding its cultural significance or use protocols. This is a Te Tiriti o Waitangi issue.

What to do about it

Do not use AI to generate, interpret, or remix mātauranga Māori or culturally significant material without appropriate guidance and authority. If your work intersects with Māori communities, engage with Māori about how AI is being used.

6
Key Landing Point
A human must always
be in the loop.

The pattern is the same across all seven risks: understand the risk, put a human safeguard in place, and take responsibility for the output.

The technology doesn't manage its own risks. It doesn't know when it's wrong, biased, or breaching someone's privacy. That's your job. Every time.
